01

Fundamentals: what every test rests on

An A/B test is a way to answer honestly: “did the change really improve something, or does it just look that way?”. Before sizing samples or reading p-values, five ideas need to be pinned down. The rest of this page builds on them.

Two hypotheses

We always compare two versions of the world. The null hypothesis H₀ says “there is no difference between the variants”. The alternative H₁ says “there is a difference”. A test does not “prove” H₁; it checks how absurd the data look if H₀ were true. If they look absurd enough, H₀ is rejected.

The four numbers that define a test

Agreed before launch

α
Significance level. The tolerated “false alarm” risk — declaring an effect that is not there. Usually 0.05 (5%).
β
Miss risk. The chance of not noticing a real effect. Usually 0.2 (20%).
1 − β
Power. The chance of catching an effect when it exists. Usually 0.8 (80%).
MDE
Minimum detectable effect. The smallest change you care to catch (say, +5% conversion). A smaller effect may be missed — that is a deliberate trade-off.

The two mistakes you can make

Reality and the test's verdict give four outcomes. Two are correct, two are errors. The whole point of planning is to keep both errors acceptable: α controls the Type I error, β the Type II.

H₀ true (no effect) H₁ true (effect exists)
Test says “effect” Type I error — false alarm. Probability = α. Correct — effect caught. Probability = 1 − β (power).
Test says “no diff” Correct — no alarm. Probability = 1 − α. Type II error — effect missed. Probability = β.

p-value: what it is and is not

A test returns a p-value: the probability of seeing a gap at least as large as observed, if H₀ were true. A small p means “data like this are rare in a world with no effect” — grounds to reject H₀.

Common mistake. A p-value is not the probability that H₀ is true, nor the probability that you are wrong. It speaks about the data given H₀, not about H₀ itself. p = 0.03 does not mean “there is a 3% chance there is no effect”.

Confidence interval

A p-value says “effect or not”; a confidence interval (CI) answers the more useful question: “how big is the effect?”. A (1 − α) interval is the range of plausible effect values. If the 95% CI for a conversion difference is [+0.4%, +2.1%] and excludes zero, the effect is significant — and you can read its size directly.

The link. A two-sided test at level α and a (1 − α) interval are two sides of one coin: an effect is significant at α exactly when the interval excludes zero. That is why the platform always shows both the p-value and the interval.

Randomisation: who lands in which variant

Fundamentals · assignment

Assignment in the platform is deterministic — no stored coin flips. The unit's identifier is hashed together with the experiment's salt, the hash maps into one of 10,000 buckets, and cumulative variant weights slice the bucket range. The same id always lands in the same variant, assignments survive retries and devices, and the split can be inspected before launch with the assignment preview.

Deterministic bucket bucket= H(id∥salt) mod 104

Where

H
a fast stable hash (uniform spreading, not cryptography)
id
the randomisation unit: user, session, or cluster id
salt
per-experiment salt — different experiments split independently
The unit matters. The unit of randomisation is the unit of independence. Randomise sessions but analyse users (or vice versa) and the observations are no longer independent — the variance is wrong. That mismatch is exactly what the ratio delta method and the cluster design effect below deal with.
02

Planning: how many users, and for how long

Sample size is computed before launch. Too small a test misses even a real effect (low power); too big wastes traffic. The formula turns your four numbers (α, power, MDE, baseline) into the observations needed per variant.

Sample size for a conversion rate (proportion)

Planning · binary metric

When the metric is “bought / not”, “clicked / not”, this is the formula. The numerator is the “noise” of two rates; the denominator is the squared effect you want to catch. The smaller the effect δ, the larger the sample (it grows as 1/δ²: half the effect → four times the sample).

Observations per variant n= ( zα 2⁢p¯(1−p¯) + zβ p0(1−p0)+p1(1−p1) ) 2 δ2

Where

p₀, p₁
baseline and variant conversion; p₁ = p₀·(1 + MDE)
δ
absolute effect, δ = p₁ − p₀
p̄
pooled rate (p₀ + p₁)/2
zα
normal quantile for α (two-sided): 1.96 at α = 0.05
zβ
quantile for power: 0.84 at power = 0.80
Baseline 10%, catching a relative +10% lift (→ 11%), α = 0.05, power 0.80 → about 14,751 users per variant.

Sample size for a mean (continuous metric)

Planning · continuous metric

When the metric is a number (revenue per user, time on site, order value), the “noise” is the spread σ. The formula is simpler: more spread and a smaller effect both grow the sample.

Observations per variant n=2 ((zα+zβ)⁢σδ) 2

Where

σ
standard deviation of the metric (spread across users)
δ
absolute effect in metric units, δ = mean·MDE
zα, zβ
the same normal quantiles as above
Mean order 100, spread σ = 40, catching +5% (δ = 5), α = 0.05, power 0.80 → about 1,005 users per variant.

The inverse question: what effect can I even detect?

Planning · MDE

Traffic is often fixed. Then it helps to invert the formula: at a fixed sample n, what is the smallest effect you can catch? For a mean this has a closed form.

Minimum detectable effect (mean) MDE=(zα+zβ)⁢σ⁢2n
Meaning. Sensitivity scales as √n: to catch an effect half the size you need four times the data. For conversions the engine solves the same equation numerically (by bisection).

Test duration

Planning · duration

Sample size is “how many users”; duration is “how many days to accrue them”. Divide the required sample by the daily traffic into the smallest variant — that arm is the bottleneck.

Days to reach the sample days= ⌈ n T⁢s⁢a⁢wmin ⌉

Where

T
expected daily traffic
s
share of audience entering the test (0…1)
a
fraction of traffic allocated (holdout / mutually-exclusive tests)
wmin
weight of the smallest variant in the split
⌈ ⌉
round up — a day is indivisible
Need 14,751 per variant, 5,000/day traffic, 100% enters the test, two equal variants (50% each) → 2,500/day into the smaller arm → 6 days.
At least a week. Even if the formula says 2–3 days, run in whole weeks: weekdays and weekends differ, and the novelty of a change distorts the first days.

Bayesian “precision” planning

Planning · Bayesian

Sometimes the goal is not “catch effect X” but “estimate a metric to within ±d”. Then the sample is driven by the desired half-width of the posterior interval, not by power. With a flat prior this reduces to almost the same normal formula.

Sample for a target precision (rate / mean) N=2⁢p(1−p)(zd)2 / N=2⁢σ2(zd)2

Where

d
target precision — the interval half-width
z
credibility quantile: 1.96 at 95%
p / σ
baseline rate or metric spread
Estimate a ~10% rate to ±1 pp at 95% → about 6,915 per variant.

Equivalence test (TOST): “prove nothing broke”

Planning · equivalence

A normal test looks for a difference. Sometimes you want the opposite: confirm a refactor, migration, or cost cut did not move the metric beyond a tolerance ±M. That is TOST (two one-sided tests). Its sample is sized separately — driven by the tolerance, not the MDE.

Sample for equivalence n=2 (σ(zα+z1−β/2)M) 2
Base 100, σ = 40, tolerance ±5% (M = 5), α = 0.05, power 0.80 → 1,097 per variant — against 1,005 for the superiority test on the same numbers. Proving “no worse” costs more.
Subtlety. Each one-sided test runs at level α (no split by 2), and the power term uses z1−β/2 because at a true zero difference the power splits symmetrically between the two bounds −M and +M.
More than two variants? When a test is sized with k > 2 variants, the platform automatically tightens the significance level to α/(k−1) — Bonferroni over the variant-vs-control comparisons — before computing the sample. The numbers already include the multiplicity price.
03

Many variants, many metrics, clusters

The moment you make more than one comparison, false alarms pile up. Test 20 metrics at 5% and, on average, one “fires” by pure chance. Here is how that is fixed — and what to do when you randomise stores or cities rather than people.

The multiple-comparisons problem

If each test is independent and runs at level α, the chance of at least one false alarm across m checks is 1 − (1 − α)m. At α = 0.05 and m = 10 that is already 40%. You control one of two quantities:

What is controlled Question Methods
FWER
family-wise error rate		 What is the chance of any false finding? Bonferroni, Holm. Stricter; loses power as m grows.
FDR
false discovery rate		 What fraction of “discoveries” is false? Benjamini–Hochberg. More powerful when metrics are many.

Bonferroni correction

Multiplicity · FWER

The simplest recipe: divide the threshold by the number of comparisons. The platform applies it automatically when there are more than two variants — that means k − 1 “variant vs control” comparisons.

Adjusted threshold αadj=αk−1 (across metrics:αadj=αm)
3 variants → 2 comparisons → threshold 0.05 / 2 = 0.025 (z rises 1.96 → 2.24). Conservative but safe.

Holm and Benjamini–Hochberg: smarter than dividing equally

Multiplicity · step procedures

Bonferroni penalises every metric equally. Step procedures sort the p-values ascending p₍₁₎ ≤ … ≤ p₍ₘ₎ and compare each with its own, progressively looser threshold.

Holm (FWER, step-down) rejectp(k)while p(k)≤αm−k+1
Benjamini–Hochberg (FDR, step-up) k∗=max⁡{k:p(k)≤km⁢q} ⇒reject ranks1…k∗

Where

m
number of metrics tested
k
rank of a p-value in the sorted list
q
tolerated false-discovery fraction (e.g. 0.05)
How to choose. Few metrics and strict control — Holm (always beats plain Bonferroni). Many metrics and a small false share is acceptable — Benjamini–Hochberg.

Cluster randomisation: the design effect and ICC

Clusters · sample size

If you assign groups to variants — stores, cities, classrooms — rather than individuals, observations inside a group resemble each other. There is less effective information than the head-count suggests, so the naive sample must be inflated by the design effect (DEFF).

Design effect (Kish) DEFF=1+(m−1)⁢ICC nclust=n×DEFF

Where

m
average cluster size (people per group)
ICC
intraclass correlation 0…1: how alike people inside a group are. 0 = independent, then DEFF = 1
n
the sample from ordinary individual randomisation
Clusters of 50, ICC = 0.02: DEFF = 1 + 49·0.02 = 1.98. A naive 1,000/variant becomes 1,980/variant ≈ 40 clusters per variant.
Watch out. Even a tiny ICC with large clusters yields a huge DEFF. This is the most common reason behind “we ran a city-level test and caught nothing”.
04

Analysing results: the test is over, now what

The data is in. Now pick a test by the design of the experiment and the shape of the metric. The platform ships twenty-two analyzers; this section walks through them by design — two independent groups, paired observations, more than two groups, time-to-event — and ends with the full table of all 22.

Two independent groups

The classic A/B: every unit sees exactly one variant. Rates take the z-test or the exact family, means take Welch and the robust quartet, event counts take Poisson, per-event ratios take the delta method.

Analysis · binary

Two-proportion z-test (conversions)

The standard “conversion A vs conversion B” test. Measure how many “standard errors” the rate difference spans. The variance uses the pooled rate p̄ — under H₀ the rates are equal.

Statistic z= p^t−p^c p¯(1−p¯)(1nc+1nt)

Where

p̂c, p̂t
observed control and treatment rates
p̄
pooled rate (all conversions / all users)
nc, nt
group sizes
Control 1,000/10,000 = 10.0%, treatment 1,120/10,000 = 11.2% → z = 2.76, p = 0.006. The difference is significant. The Wilson interval for the treatment rate: [10.60%; 11.83%].
Interval for a proportion: why Wilson, not Wald

The naive interval p̂ ± z·√(p̂(1−p̂)/n) (Wald) misbehaves near the edges (0 or 1) and at small n — collapsing to zero width at p̂ = 0. The platform uses the Wilson score interval (inverting the score test): always inside [0, 1], with honest coverage. For the difference of rates, the Newcombe hybrid of two Wilson intervals.

Wilson score interval p^+z22n±zp^(1−p^)n+z24n2 1+z2/n
Analysis · binary, exact

Small counts: Fisher, Boschloo, Barnard

The z-test's normal approximation gets shaky when successes are scarce — a handful of conversions, near-zero rates. Exact tests compute the p-value directly from the 2×2 table. Fisher conditions on both margins (hypergeometric). Boschloo and Barnard are unconditional: they maximise over the unknown common rate π, which makes them strictly more powerful than Fisher on typical A/B data. The engine caps table sizes for the exact computations (500k totals for Fisher; a shared cap for the unconditional pair).

Fisher: hypergeometric probability of a table P(a)= C(m1,a)⁢C(m2,c) C(n,a+c)
Unconditional (Boschloo / Barnard): maximise over the nuisance rate p= maxπ ∑T⪰obs Pπ(T)

Where

m₁, m₂
row totals — the two group sizes
a, c
successes in control and treatment
π
the unknown common rate under H₀ — the “nuisance” parameter
T ⪰ obs
tables at least as extreme as observed: Boschloo orders them by Fisher's p, Barnard by the pooled Wald z
3/40 (7.5%) vs 11/40 (27.5%): Fisher p = 0.0367; Boschloo p = 0.0206; Barnard p = 0.0204 (ordering z = −2.354). The unconditional pair finds the same data roughly twice as convincing.
Which one? Reach for an exact test when any expected cell is small (rule of thumb: below 5). Between them, Boschloo is the pragmatic default — never less powerful than Fisher. Barnard can squeeze out slightly more power but costs more to compute.
Analysis · continuous

Welch's t-test (means)

Comparing means when the spread may differ between groups (the usual case). Unlike the classic t-test, Welch does not assume equal variances — which makes it the sensible default.

Statistic and degrees of freedom (Welch–Satterthwaite) t= x¯t−x¯c sc2nc+st2nt ν= (sc2/nc+st2/nt)2 (sc2/nc)2nc−1+(st2/nt)2nt−1

Where

x̄c, x̄t
group means
sc, st
group standard deviations
ν
effective degrees of freedom — the Student-t reference uses these
Control 100 ± 40 (n = 1,005), treatment 105 ± 41 (n = 1,005) → t = 2.77, ν ≈ 2,007, p ≈ 0.006. Effect magnitude is reported alongside as Cohen's d = difference / pooled spread.
Analysis · robust

Heavy tails and outliers: the robust quartet

Revenue-like metrics — skew, heavy tails, outliers — break the t-test's assumptions. The engine ships four tests that do not trust the shape of the distribution, shown here on one and the same dataset (n = 10 per group) so their outputs can be compared side by side. Mann–Whitney compares by ranks; bootstrap / permutation resamples the observed data instead of assuming a curve; the quantile test moves the question to the median (or any percentile); Yuen's trimmed t cuts the extreme 20% of each tail before comparing means.

One dataset for all four: C = [12, 15, 9, 22, 30, 14, 18, 25, 11, 16], T = [18, 24, 13, 29, 41, 19, 26, 33, 15, 22].

Permutation p-value and percentile bootstrap CI pperm= 1+#{|Δ∗|≥|Δ^|} B+1 CI95=[Δ(2.5%)∗;Δ(97.5%)∗]

Analysis · counts

Event counts: Poisson rate test

When the metric is a count of events over exposure — errors per thousand sessions, orders per user-day — compare intensities, not shares. The engine uses the exact conditional test: given the total number of events, the treatment's share of them is Binomial under H₀ (exact up to 1M events). The effect is a rate ratio, with a CI for the rate difference.

Conditional binomial and the rate ratio Xt∣(Xt+Xc) ∼Bin(Xt+Xc,etet+ec) RR=Xt/etXc/ec

Where

Xc, Xt
event counts in control and treatment
ec, et
exposures (users, sessions, user-days)
RR
rate ratio — the multiplicative effect
46 events / 11,500 vs 72 / 11,800: rate 0.40% → 0.61%, ratio 1.525, exact p = 0.0269, CI for the difference [0.0003; 0.0039].
Analysis · ratio

Ratio metrics: the delta method

CTR, “clicks per impression”, “revenue per session” are a ratio R = ΣY / ΣX where the user is randomised but counting is by events. The naive variance here is wrong: it ignores the within-user link between numerator and denominator. The delta method linearises the ratio and recovers the correct variance.

Variance of the ratio (delta method) Var(R^)= σY2−2R^σXY+R^2σX2 n⁢μX2

Where

μX
mean denominator per user
σ²X, σ²Y
per-user numerator and denominator variances
σXY
their covariance — exactly what the naive formula drops
From the engine's frozen tests: clicks-per-impression 0.436 → 0.538, Δ = 0.1018, z = 8.74 — a strongly significant ratio lift, with the within-user covariance properly accounted for.

Paired observations

The same units measured twice — before/after, or matched pairs. Pairing cancels the between-user variance inside each pair, which is why paired designs reach significance on a fraction of the sample. The pair, not the user, is the unit of analysis.

Analysis · paired means

Paired t-test

Take the within-pair differences d and test whether their mean is zero. All the stable between-user spread cancels inside each pair; only the change itself carries noise. The effect size is Cohen's dz — in units of the differences' spread.

Statistic and paired effect size t=d¯sd/n df=n−1 dz=d¯sd

Where

d̄
mean of the within-pair differences
sd
standard deviation of the differences
dz
paired effect size: mean difference over its spread
12 before/after pairs (309.2 → 318.3): Δ = +9.17, t = 6.46, df 11, p < 0.001, CI [6.04; 12.29], dz = 1.87. Two independent groups of 12 would never see this.
Analysis · paired, ranks

Wilcoxon signed-rank

The nonparametric twin of the paired t: rank the absolute differences, sum the ranks of the positive and the negative ones separately, test the smaller sum (normal approximation for the reference). Robust to outliers among the differences; the effect is the Hodges–Lehmann pseudomedian of the pair differences.

Statistic and normal approximation W=min⁡(W+,W−) z=W−μWσW
The same 12 pairs: W = 1, z = −2.945, p = 0.0032, pseudomedian +9.5 [6; 12.5].
Analysis · paired, binary

McNemar: paired rates

For paired binary outcomes (converted before vs after, same users) only the discordant pairs carry information: b pairs switched 0→1, c pairs switched 1→0; concordant pairs cancel out. The engine applies the continuity correction and reports the odds ratio b/c and the difference in rates.

Continuity-corrected statistic and odds ratio χ2= (|b−c|−1)2 b+c OR=bc

Where

b
pairs that improved (0→1)
c
pairs that worsened (1→0)
1,000 pairs, b = 30, c = 12: χ² = 6.881 (corrected), p = 0.0087, OR = 2.5, Δ = +1.8 pp [0.53; 3.07].

More than two groups, and categories

With k variants, run one omnibus test first — “is anything different at all?” — and only then pairwise comparisons with a multiplicity correction (section 03). Jumping straight into pairwise tests multiplies false alarms.

Analysis · k groups

Omnibus: Welch ANOVA and Kruskal–Wallis

Welch ANOVA compares k means without assuming equal variances (the same philosophy as Welch's t). Kruskal–Wallis is its rank-based twin. Effect sizes: η² and ε² — the share of variance explained by group membership.

Welch ANOVA (unequal variances) wi=nisi2 F∗= ∑iwi(x¯i−x¯w)2/(k−1) D
Kruskal–Wallis and its effect size H= 12N(N+1) ∑iRi2ni −3(N+1) ε2=H−k+1N−k

Where

k, N
number of groups and total sample size
x̄w, D
variance-weighted grand mean; D is Welch's denominator correcting for unequal variances — the reference is F(k−1, df) with Welch's df
Ri
sum of ranks of group i in the pooled sample
η², ε²
explained-variance shares: η² for ANOVA, ε² for Kruskal–Wallis
3 groups × 10 (the third one lower): Welch ANOVA F* = 3.58, df (2; 16.87), p = 0.0506 — not significant; Kruskal–Wallis on the same data H = 6.44, p = 0.0399 — significant, ε² = 0.222.
An instructive split. The same data, two defensible tests, opposite verdicts at α = 0.05 (0.0506 vs 0.0399). Heavy tails weaken the mean-based test but barely touch the rank-based one. Treat p-values near the threshold as borderline evidence, not binary truth — and fix the test family before looking at the data.
Analysis · categorical

Categories: χ² r×c, Cramér V, G-test

When the outcome is one of several categories (chosen tariff, click target) across several variants, the χ² independence test asks whether the distribution differs by variant, and Cramér V (0…1) says how strongly. The G-test is the likelihood-ratio version of the same question — same answer on large tables, and additive across breakdowns.

Independence test and association size χ2= ∑i,j (Oij−Eij)2Eij V=χ2n⁢min⁡(r−1,c−1)
G-test (likelihood ratio) G=2 ∑i,j Oij⁢ln⁡OijEij

Where

Oij, Eij
observed and expected counts; E = (row total × column total) / n
r, c
numbers of rows and columns
V
association size: 0 — independent, 1 — perfectly determined
3 tariffs × 3 variants [[420, 380, 200], [410, 360, 230], [380, 340, 280]]: χ² = 18.17, df 4, p = 0.0011; G = 18.07, p = 0.0012; Cramér V = 0.055 — significant, yet the association is weak. With n ≈ 3,000 significance is cheap; V keeps you honest.
Cochran's rule. The χ² approximation wants expected counts of at least 5 in (nearly) every cell — here min E = 236.7, fine. With sparse cells, collapse categories or use an exact test.

Time-to-event (survival)

Sometimes the metric is “how long until the event” — churn, first purchase, activation — and at the end of the window some users have not had the event yet. Dropping them biases everything; survival methods keep them as censored: they contribute “survived at least this long”.

Analysis · survival

Kaplan–Meier: the survival curve

The product-limit estimator builds S(t) — the probability of surviving past t — by multiplying, at every observed event time, the fraction of those still at risk who did not have the event. Censored users leave the risk set without counting as events.

Product-limit estimator S^(t)= ∏ti≤t (1−dini)

Where

ti
distinct event times
di
events at time ti
ni
units at risk just before ti
Control churn curve from the dataset below: Ŝ(25) = 0.25 [0.005; 0.495] — by day 25 only a quarter is retained, and at this sample size the interval is honest about how little that pins down.
Analysis · survival

Log-rank and Fleming–Harrington

The log-rank test compares whole curves, not one point: at every event time it tallies observed vs expected events in group 1 (Mantel–Cox O − E over the variance V). Fleming–Harrington G(ρ,γ) is the weighted family: ρ > 0 emphasises early differences, γ > 0 late ones; G(0,0) is exactly log-rank. For k groups the statistic is the quadratic form zᵀV⁻¹z ~ χ²(k−1).

Log-rank (Mantel–Cox) χ2= (O1−E1)2 V
Fleming–Harrington weights and the k-sample form w(t)= S^(t−)ρ (1−S^(t−))γ χ2=zTV−1z

Where

O₁, E₁
observed and expected events in group 1, summed over event times
V
hypergeometric variance of O₁
ρ, γ
the weighting knobs: (0,0) — classic; ρ > 0 — early; γ > 0 — late differences
Churn days C = [3, 5, 7, 9, 12, 14, 18, 21, 25] + 3 censored at day 30 vs T = [6, 10, 15, 22, 28] + 7 censored: χ² = 3.32, p = 0.068 (O₁ = 9 vs E₁ = 5.68). G(0,0) reproduces it exactly.
Analysis · survival

Cox proportional hazards

The Cox model turns the group difference into a hazard ratio: HR = eβ multiplies the instantaneous risk of the event at every moment. The engine fits β for the treatment indicator by partial likelihood and tests it with the Wald z; the baseline hazard h₀(t) stays unspecified.

Model, effect, and Wald test h(t∣x)= h0(t)⁢ eβx HR=eβ^ z=β^SE(β^)

Where

h₀(t)
baseline hazard — never estimated; cancels out of the partial likelihood
x
treatment indicator (0 = control, 1 = treatment)
HR
hazard ratio: HR < 1 — the event is slowed (good for churn), HR > 1 — accelerated
Same dataset: HR = 0.372 [0.123; 1.123], β = −0.988, SE 0.563, p = 0.079. The point estimate says “churn hazard almost ×3 lower”; the interval says the data cannot yet back that up. Effect size and significance are different questions.
PH assumption. “Proportional hazards” means the ratio is constant over time; if the curves cross, HR averages a sign change — look at the KM curves first. On degenerate data with a monotone likelihood (e.g. all events in one arm) the engine refuses to fit rather than report a fake HR.

Live monitoring: while the test runs

Planning is not the end. While the experiment runs you must catch randomisation faults, avoid “peeking” the wrong way, speed up conclusions by cutting variance, and make sure a win on the primary metric does not break everything else.

SRM: did randomisation break

Monitoring · health

Sample Ratio Mismatch — when the actual traffic split (say 50/50) diverges from the expected one beyond chance. A red flag: a broken splitter, dropped events, bots. With SRM the results are not trustworthy — fix the cause. Detected by a chi-square goodness-of-fit test.

Goodness-of-fit statistic χ2=∑i=1k(Oi−Ei)2Ei df=k−1

Where

Oi
observed count in variant i
Ei
expected = share·total
k
number of variants
Expect 50/50 of 20,000, see 10,300 / 9,700 → χ² = 18.0, p ≈ 0.00002. The alert threshold p < 0.001 is crossed → SRM, stop.

The peeking problem and group-sequential design

Monitoring · early stops

Peek at the p-value daily and stop the moment it dips below 0.05 — and the true false-alarm rate is not 5% but several times higher. Every “look” is a fresh chance to err. O'Brien–Fleming boundaries fix this: early looks demand a near-impossible z, the threshold relaxes toward the end, and the total α stays 0.05.

Boundary at information fraction t zk=zfinaltk tk=looktotal looks
With 5 planned looks, the first (t = 0.2) needs ≈ z = 4.5 (p ≈ 0.000007): stopping that early is nearly impossible. That guards against random early “wins”. The price — the planned sample grows slightly (× ~1.03, the engine interpolates the calibrated final anchor).

Always-valid: look whenever you like

Monitoring · anytime

O'Brien–Fleming needs pre-planned looks. On a live dashboard the looks are not planned. Then use always-valid inference (a mixture mSPRT): the p-value and interval stay correct under continuous monitoring — any number of times, at any moment.

Mixture likelihood ratio and anytime-p Λn=VV+τ2⁢exp⁡(τ2θ^22V(V+τ2)) p=min⁡(1,1/Λn)

Where

θ̂
observed effect (difference of means / rates)
V
variance of the effect estimate
τ²
the mixture spread — tuned to the expected effect; does not affect validity, only power
Confidence sequence. The method has a companion interval θ̂ ± r that covers the true effect with probability 1 − α simultaneously at all sample sizes. It excludes zero exactly when the anytime-p drops below α.

The Bayesian view: P(B > A)

Monitoring · Bayesian

Instead of “significant / not”, the Bayesian view answers the direct business question: what is the probability that B beats A? For conversions, take a Beta posterior per variant and count how often a draw of B exceeds a draw of A. The engine does this by Monte-Carlo simulation with a fixed seed — reproducible, not a closed form.

Posterior and probability of superiority pi∼Beta(1+xi,1+ni−xi) P(B>A)=P(pB>pA)

Where

xi, ni
conversions and size of variant i
Beta(1+x, 1+n−x)
posterior under a uniform Beta(1,1) prior
Not the same as a p-value. P(B > A) = 97% is not “1 − p”. It is the probability that B is better, given the data and prior. Handy for decisions, but it needs the same stopping discipline as the frequentist path.

CUPED: less noise, faster conclusions

Monitoring · variance reduction

Part of a metric's spread is explained by what the user was like before the test. CUPED subtracts that predictable part using a pre-experiment covariate X (say, past activity). Variance drops by ρ² — the test gains power with zero extra users.

Adjusted metric and variance reduction Ycuped=Y−θ(X−X¯), θ=cov(Y,X)var(X) varnewvarold=1−ρ2

Where

X
pre-experiment covariate (independent of the variant!)
θ
optimal coefficient = cov(Y,X)/var(X)
ρ
correlation of Y and X
Correlation ρ = 0.6 → variance falls by 36%, the standard error by 20%. Equivalent to a ~1.5× larger sample, for free.
In the engine: several covariates. The platform's CUPED is multi-covariate: θ is a vector solved from the covariance system (a small linear solve), so several pre-experiment signals can subtract noise together. The formula above is the one-covariate case.

Post-stratification: estimate inside strata, then combine

Monitoring · variance reduction

If traffic is a mix of known strata with different baselines — platform, country, new vs returning — estimate the effect inside each stratum and combine with size weights. Randomisation imbalance across strata stops leaking into the effect estimate: a cheap, assumption-light cousin of CUPED.

Size-weighted combination Δ^= ∑s wsΔ^s Var= ∑s ws2Vars

Where

s
stratum index
ws
stratum share of traffic, Σw = 1
Δ̂s, Vars
per-stratum effect estimate and its variance
Stratum A: 6,000 users, Δ = 1.2 pp, var 3·10⁻⁵; stratum B: 4,000 users, Δ = 1.8 pp, var 5·10⁻⁵ → combined Δ = 1.44 pp, z = 3.32, p = 0.0009, CI [0.59; 2.29] pp.

Guardrail metrics: a win must not break anything

Monitoring · protection

A variant can win on conversion yet inflate load time, payment errors, or unsubscribes. A guardrail asks a one-sided question: “did the variant move the metric the harmful way beyond a tolerable margin M?”. This is a non-inferiority test, not a search for a difference.

One-sided breach test z=harm−MVar(Δ) p=1−Φ(z)

Where

harm
the shift in the harmful direction: +Δ if an increase is bad (latency), −Δ if a drop is bad (revenue)
M
tolerable harm margin (0 = any significant harm is a breach)
Φ
standard normal CDF
Latency rose +15 ms (SE = 2), tolerance M = 10 ms → z = (15 − 10)/2 = 2.5, p ≈ 0.006 → margin breached. Between “ok” and “breached” sits a yellow “warning” zone: the estimate is past the margin but not significantly.

Data hygiene: what breaks tests before any statistics

Monitoring · health

No formula repairs broken data. Four habits protect an experiment long before p-values enter the picture:

1
Identity resolution. One person = one unit. If the same user re-enters as web + app + incognito, both arms get polluted and the measured effect washes out.
2
Late events. Conversions trail clicks by hours or days; cutting the window at the stop moment silently biases against the newer arm. Freeze one attribution window and apply it to both arms symmetrically.
3
Bot filtering. Bots convert at ~0% and inflate denominators — asymmetrically, whenever targeting differs between arms. Filter before analysis, by the same rule everywhere.
4
Holdout. A small always-control slice measures the long-term, cumulative effect of shipping many “winners” — and catches winner's-curse inflation.

When not to A/B: Thompson sampling

Monitoring · bandits

A fixed test spends half the traffic on the loser until the very end. When the goal is to maximise reward during a short window (promo banners, subject lines) rather than to measure the effect precisely, a bandit reallocates traffic as evidence accumulates. Thompson sampling: draw a plausible rate for each arm from its Beta posterior and serve the arm that won the draw. The platform ships this as a planning simulator (Beta-Bernoulli Monte-Carlo), not a live traffic allocator.

One allocation step θ∼i∼Beta(1+xi,1+ni−xi) a∗=argmaxiθ∼i
The price. A bandit is not a free upgrade: losing arms keep noisy, biased estimates; peeking rules do not transfer; and decision-grade measurement still wants a fixed test. Exploration costs measurement.

Healthy-test checklist

1
Hypothesis and metric fixed before launch — including the MDE and the primary metric.
2
Sample size computed, duration in whole weeks.
3
No SRM — the traffic split matches the plan.
4
Multiplicity correction if more than one metric or variant.
5
Guardrail metrics watched — the win does not break latency, errors, revenue.
6
Correct stopping: planned horizon, sequential boundaries, or always-valid — not “it dipped below 0.05”.
7
Decide on the interval, not the p-value alone — effect size matters.
8
Data hygiene checked — one identity per user, a symmetric attribution window, bots filtered, a holdout in place.
